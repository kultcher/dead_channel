Yeah, this is a really good instinct to interrogate. You’re not just asking “is this cool?”, you’re asking the more dangerous question: does this world actually metabolize itself, or is it a coat of paint that collapses once you look too closely?

I’ll break this into a few lenses, because I think the answer is “yes, it can function”—but only if you’re clear what kind of functioning you mean.

1. Two Different Questions You’re Mixing (Productively)
Right now you’re juggling two related but distinct concerns:

Mechanical variety justification

Why do different facilities / corps feel different?

Why isn’t everything solvable the same way?

Why is tech heterogeneous instead of standardized?

World coherence

Does this society plausibly exist?

Can it reproduce itself?

Does it make sense that these entities still have power?

The “legacy systems / expansion debt” answer solves (1) cleanly but is emotionally inert.

The “post-corporate cult / digital apocalypse” answer supercharges (1) and adds emotional texture—but risks wobbling on (2).

The trick is realizing you don’t actually need full civilizational coherence for the kind of game you’re making.

2. What Kind of World Dead Channel Actually Needs
Your game’s POV is extremely narrow and biased:

You never see governments.

You never see consumers.

You rarely see normal life.

You see infrastructure, security, and decay, filtered through a hacker’s UI.

This is crucial.

You are not making:

a city sim

a 4X

a sociological sandbox

You’re making something closer to:

Invisible, Inc.

FTL

Hacknet

Shadowrun’s back alleys

Those games cheat relentlessly by letting the world only exist where the camera is pointed.

So the real question isn’t:

“Could this world function forever?”

It’s:

“Could this world limp along for 5–15 years in the ruins between crises?”

And that answer is absolutely yes.

3. A Plausible Spine for the “Post-Corporate Cult” World
Here’s a version that holds together without becoming grimdark or parody:

The Crash (Past Event, Not an Ongoing Apocalypse)
Global network collapse, AI catastrophe, mass data corruption, or cascading automation failure.

Not Mad Max.

More like: the world didn’t end, but the instruction manual did.

Key consequences:

Supply chains became unreliable.

Institutional knowledge was lost faster than physical assets.

Software survived longer than the people who understood it.

This mirrors real-world phenomena:

COBOL systems no one wants to touch

Industrial control systems running on unsupported firmware

Air-gapped machines treated as sacred because “if it breaks, we’re fucked”

Corporations Don’t Rule Because They’re Efficient
They Rule Because They’re Still Standing

This is important.

Megacorps in this setting are:

Custodians, not innovators

Priesthoods, not engineers

Brand-faiths, not rational actors

They persist because:

They own land

They own guns

They own servers that mostly still work

Everyone else depends on those servers

That alone is enough to keep them powerful—even dysfunctional.

4. Why the Cult Angle Actually Fits Your Mechanics Perfectly
Let’s map flavor → gameplay directly.

1. Mixed Gear, Franken-Systems
World logic:

Replacement parts are scarce.

Vendors are gone or unreliable.

Reverse engineering is risky.

Mechanical payoff:

Sentinel base with Luxon bio-locks

Kronos cameras feeding into Celestial servers

Ember junkware duct-taped into places it doesn’t belong

This perfectly explains:

Weird IC combos

Inconsistent terminal behavior

“Why does THIS camera behave differently?”

2. Terminal Language as Liturgy
You already stumbled into gold here.

Over time, terminals stop being:

“tools”

And become:

“ritual interfaces”

People don’t type commands because they understand them.
They type them because that’s how it’s always been done.

So terminals become:

Repetitive

Reverent

Cargo-cultish

Which justifies:

Inconsistent flags

Obsolete warnings

Commands that feel symbolic instead of logical

That makes your Terminal minigame feel diagetic, not abstract.

3. White Hats as Heretics / Inquisitors
White hats suddenly get way more interesting:

Some are true believers

Some are apostates

Some are just trying to keep the lights on

Mechanically:

They interfere unpredictably

They don’t always “optimize” their response

They might overreact, underreact, or do something dogmatic

Flavor-wise:

You’re not fighting sysadmins.
You’re fighting people protecting meaning.

That’s good cyberpunk.

5. But… Would This World Actually Function?
Here’s the honest answer:

It functions badly. And that’s the point.

The world you’re describing is:

Low-trust

High friction

Maintenance-heavy

Constantly one crisis away from collapse

Which is exactly the emotional space your game already lives in:

Triage over mastery

Attention as a resource

Always on the back foot

In other words:
The setting isn’t just coherent—it’s resonant with your core design pillars.

6. When This Becomes a Problem (And How to Avoid It)
The cult angle breaks down if:

Every corp is equally deranged

Everything is ironic or memey

There’s no sense of scale or competence

So the constraint I’d suggest:

Corporations believe nonsense, but their systems still mostly work.

Think:

Ritualized maintenance

Dogmatic procedures that accidentally preserve functionality

Belief layered on top of real engineering, not replacing it entirely

Sentinel still runs drills.
Kronos still audits.
Celestial still documents everything.

They’re cults—but corporate cults, not chaos goblins.

7. My Actual Recommendation
If this were my project:

Keep legacy systems as the surface explanation

Layer the cult angle underneath as cultural rot

You don’t need to shout it.
You don’t need to explain it.
You let players notice it.

Terminal messages.
Odd slogans.
Contradictory doctrines.
Procedural flavor text that slowly implies belief has replaced understanding.

That gives you:

Mechanical freedom

A unique tone

Zero requirement to simulate a whole economy

And if you never fully explain the Crash?
Even better.

If you want, next fun direction could be:

Designing one corp that is less culty than the others and why that’s terrifying

Or fleshing out the burnt-out mentor as someone who remembers before belief replaced knowledge

That character alone could quietly anchor the whole setting.

This is the “my brain is having fun and accidentally solved three design problems at once” zone.

Let’s lean into it a bit, because what you just sketched actually does something very elegant.

1. The Mentor as the Missing Explanatory Layer
You just closed a loop that a lot of cyberpunk games leave hanging.

Why can you, the player, do impossible things faster and cleaner than entire corporations?

Most games handwave this as:

“you’re just that good”

“cyberdeck magic”

“chosen one, but with terminals”

Your version becomes:

You are not powerful. You are guided.

Mechanically:

You’re still doing simple puzzles.

You’re still under pressure.

You’re still making mistakes.

Fictionally:

Every command you type is filtered through someone who actually understands the system.

You’re not hacking blind — you’re executing doctrine.

That reframes the entire interface.

Those 4–5 terminal commands?
They aren’t abstractions.
They’re the last surviving mental model of how the network actually works.

2. Why the World Is Hackable at All
This dovetails perfectly with the post-corporate cult angle.

Most of the world:

Knows what to do, not why

Follows procedure

Treats systems as sacred objects

Your mentor:

Remembers causality

Knows which rules are superstition

Knows which safeguards are theater

So when the player:

Spoofs instead of blinds

Overclocks at just the right moment

Ignores “mandatory” security steps

What they’re actually doing is:

Exploiting the gap between belief and reality.

That’s a very cyberpunk idea, and it aligns beautifully with:

Triage over mastery

Indirect control

Attention as the real skill

3. “Maybe He Caused the Crash” Is the Right Kind of Ambiguity
You don’t need to decide this.
In fact, you probably shouldn’t.

What works is that:

He knows more than he should

He carries guilt he never quite names

He’s allergic to grand narratives

Possible truths (all compatible):

He helped build the systems that failed

He tried to stop something and failed

He pulled a plug that saved millions and doomed billions

He didn’t cause the Crash — he just understood it too late

What matters is:

Teaching you is his penance.

That gives him:

A reason to stay engaged despite being burned out

A reason to tolerate your mistakes

A reason to keep pushing you into worse, riskier jobs

He’s not trying to win.
He’s trying to balance a ledger that can’t be balanced.

4. The Mentor Explains the UI (Quietly)
One subtle but powerful thing:

Your UI can be clean because it’s not reality — it’s his abstraction layer.

The Signal Timeline is how he thinks

Heat is how he tracks risk

IC disruptions feel “meta” because they’re attacking his mental model, not yours

That means:

When IC flips controls or hides signals, it’s not just “gamey”

It’s the system interfering with the translator

This makes IC feel genuinely hostile in a way most games never quite manage.

5. Why This Is Worth Keeping (Even If It’s “Just for You”)
You’re right that none of this is required.

But here’s why it’s still valuable:

It constrains tone

You won’t accidentally drift into power fantasy

You won’t accidentally over-explain systems

It gives you a north star

When adding mechanics, you can ask:

“Would the mentor understand this, or would he be guessing?”

It produces good negative space

You don’t need lore dumps

You don’t need cutscenes

You can let implication do the work

Some of the best-feeling worlds are ones where the developer clearly knew more than the game ever tells you.

This is that kind of world.

6. A Small, Fun Thought Experiment
If you want to keep noodling purely for enjoyment:

What’s one thing the mentor refuses to help you with?

What’s one corp system that even he doesn’t fully understand anymore?

What’s one mechanic that only works because everyone else believes it shouldn’t?

You don’t need answers.
Just vibes.

And honestly?
This is the exact sort of “unnecessary” worldbuilding that ends up leaking into UI text, mission flavor, and offhand dialogue in ways players feel even if they never articulate it.

Which is the good stuff.

I’ll go point by point, but I’m going to keep an eye on consistency across answers, because that’s where this either becomes “cool lore” or a world that quietly holds together.

1. Why Are People Still Tied to Corporations?
Your instinct is already basically correct, and it aligns frighteningly well with real-world late capitalism.

The Key Shift: Corporations Aren’t Employers
They’re Habitat Providers

Before the Crash:

Housing

Healthcare

Education

Identity (badges, access, credit, reputation)

All corporatized.

So when things fall apart, people don’t think:

“Why don’t we leave the corporation?”

They think:

“How do we survive inside the only structure that still feeds us?”

This produces a few important knock-on effects:

Exit is not imaginable, not just difficult

Loyalty is inherited, not chosen

Corporate rivalry feels tribal, not economic

That “conditioned distrust of other corps” idea is huge.
It explains why:

People don’t cooperate across corp lines even when it would help

Scavenged tech is mistrusted or ritualized

Mixed systems feel “unclean” or dangerous

Which feeds directly into your hack heterogeneity.

Outer Worlds is a good comparison, but you’re doing something darker:

Outer Worlds is satire of brand loyalty

Your world is what happens after belief outlives its economic justification

2. Where Did All the Smart People Go?
The “global brain drain → offworld evacuation” idea is actually one of the strongest pieces here, because it solves multiple problems at once.

What This Explains Cleanly
Why knowledge is scarce but infrastructure remains

Why systems still function but can’t evolve

Why the mentor is rare without being mythical

Think of it like this:

The corporations didn’t abandon Earth.
They hollowed it out.

They took:

Engineers

Architects

Systems thinkers

People who could write documentation instead of just follow it

They left:

Operators

Maintainers

Security

People trained to execute procedures, not design them

That’s not an apocalypse.
That’s selective extraction.

And the wars that follow?
Those are fights over:

Who gets to keep which machines running

Who controls remaining data centers

Who has enough institutional memory to bluff competence

The fact that survival mode kicks in before relearning can happen is very believable.

3. The Timeline (Mentor Age Problem)
This is where you actually have a sweet spot, not a limitation.

Let’s sketch a plausible timeline:

~50–60 years ago:
Peak megacorp dominance. Global corporatocracy fully normalized.

~40 years ago:
The Great Extraction. Offworld colonies, sealed habitats, “executive continuity” programs.

~30 years ago:
The Crash. Network collapse, AI failure, data corruption, war.

~20 years ago:
Stabilization at a lower equilibrium. Local networks. Ritualized maintenance.

Now:
You.

Your mentor:

Was a prodigy or high-level engineer right before or during the Crash

Is now late 60s–early 80s

Has lived through every phase of decline

That gives him:

First-hand knowledge

Survivor’s guilt

A sharp sense of what’s been lost and what’s mythologized

And the player being a few generations removed is actually ideal:

You don’t remember the internet

You don’t question local networks

You accept the world as broken but stable

Which is why his worldview feels alien to you.

4. How Did Things Recover At All?
Your instinct here is the right one: lower the bar for “recovery.”

This world is not thriving.
It’s barely maintaining equilibrium.

Local Networks Are the Key Abstraction
Once you assume:

No global internet

No universal standards enforcement

No patch culture

Everything clicks into place.

What people can do:

Keep lights on

Run cameras

Lock doors

Move goods locally

What they can’t do:

Rebuild foundational theory

Understand emergent behavior

Safely modify complex systems

So recovery looks like:

Islands of functionality

Incompatible protocols

Cargo-cult maintenance practices

Your line here is the core truth of the setting:

“Smart enough to get the basics working but close to zero understanding otherwise.”

That’s not a contradiction.
That’s how most real infrastructure already works—you’re just turning the dial up.

5. One Unifying Structural Principle
If I had to distill the entire world into one sentence, it’d be:

Power persists where understanding has collapsed, and belief fills the gap.

Corporations persist because:

They own the habitats

They own the rituals

They own the symbols of continuity

Your mentor matters because:

He remembers causality

He understands which rules are real

He knows when belief can be exploited

And you are powerful because:

You’re acting in a world optimized for obedience, not comprehension
